About Me

I received my PhD degree from Uppsala University in June 2024. The main focus of my thesis was on solving practical problems that occur when deploying multiple backscatter tags. I have eight years of experience working with low power communication technologies. I love building systems to creatively solve real world problems.

I'm a long-term collaborator with Microsoft Research. I have spent time as a Google summer intern and a research intern at SCoRe lab. Prior to starting my PhD, I was a Software Engineer at the London Stock Exchange Group, where I developed software for mission-critical systems. I received my Bachelor's degree in Computer Science from the University of Colombo.
One piece of advice I would give to my younger self: "Your creativity is a gift, use it more boldly!"

I'm on the academic job market.

Selected Projects

  • Low-power Transmitters: Low-power wireless technologies such as backscatter communication have revolutionized battery-free sensing by enabling data transmission with very low energy consumption. Unlike conventional wireless transmitters, backscatter devices eliminate the need to generate a carrier signal, instead modulating information onto existing RF waves from dedicated or ambient sources such as RFID readers, TV towers, or FM broadcasts. In this work, we overcome this constraint and demonstrate that wireless data transmission is possible without relying on any generated or ambient RF source. Leveraging the unique properties of tunnel diodes, we design an ultra-low-power signal generator capable of producing a stable, harmonic-free carrier that can be modulated for long-range communication. Our prototype achieves kilometre-scale transmission while consuming only microwatts of power at the front end, paving the way for a new class of self-sustaining, zero-energy wireless systems.
  • Analog Backscatter: Analog backscatter tags are highly resource-constrained and lack essential network functionalities such as identification, querying and channel sensing mechanisms. In the first project we introduced a novel harmonics-based tag identification method, based on musical instrument identification. Backscatter tags are known for their frequency agnostic behaviour, and reflect any given carrier frequency, posing limitations to identification and querying when multiple tags are active. To overcome this, in a follow-up work we develop a desynchronization technique that enables decoding of individual tag signals. Analog backscatter tags typically encode sensor data as frequency shifts of the carrier signal. Therefore, representing high bandwidth sensor data with high resolution demands increased power due to high-frequency switching at the tag. We propose and demonstrate a system that uses higher harmonic expansion to achieve high-resolution sensing without raising the tag's power consumption, enabling portable, low-cost setups with receivers like an RTL-SDR dongle. In another project we implemented channel estimation mechanisms for analog tags and used them for external RF source localization.
  • Digital Backscatter: Digital backscatter tags are capable of communicating with packets, allowing seamless integration into standards-based networks. However, achieving multi-hop is challenging due to limited transmission range and the low-power nature of receiver tags.We demonstrate the first multi-hop tag-to-tag backscatter network where tags can decode, modify, and forward packets. The tag-to-tag network consists of tags capable of both transmitting and receiving packets, allowing multi-hop communication that extends range and improves reliability in packet reception. Another project I worked on is carrier-scheduling. Interoperability between backscatter devices and commodity networks requires efficient carrier provisioning, to enhance network scalability and performance. We developed a fast carrier scheduling algorithm and a system that leverages standard sensor nodes to provide the unmodulated carrier needed by battery-free tags. The system optimally allocates carriers to backscatter tags, minimizing interference and maximizing throughput in dense deployments.
  • Security: Low-power devices like backscatter tags trade off complexity for low power consumption, limiting the use of encryption and exposing systems to security threats. RF fingerprinting, which requires no additional resources, aligns with the low-power design philosophy of backscatter systems. In this project we applied RF fingerprinting with machine learning for digital backscatter source identification.
